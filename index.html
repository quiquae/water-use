AI and Water Use: Investigating the Estimates

How much water does a ChatGPT query actually use? It depends on who you ask. Sam Altman says a single Chat-GPT query uses "one-fifteenth of a teaspoon" of water (Altman). According to UC Riverside and UT Arlington researchers, taking into account other types of water used in cooling the data center, generating its electricity, and creating the chips and materials produces an estimate closer to 3.5 teaspoons per query, about fifty times the number suggested by Altman (Li et al.). Huge proportions of Americans report being deeply concerned about AI's impact on water consumption (McDermott and Sanders) and oppose data centers being built near them (Data Center Watch Report). Yet how much water data centers actually use, and how important this consumption is, can be extremely difficult to pin down.

Try the guessing game above, then check the "Compare Estimates" tab to see why the numbers vary so much.

A note on methodology: This tool aims to use full lifecycle estimates (all three scopes) for both AI queries and comparison items where possible. Consumer product data comes from the Water Footprint Network, which tracks water from raw materials through manufacturing. However, not all items have complete scope data available—some sources only report direct water use or don't specify their methodology. I've cited sources for every comparison so you can check what's included in each estimate. When in doubt, hover over items to see their water amounts and click through to the source links.

How This Tool Works

I built this interactive tool to help people understand AI water consumption through direct comparison. You can:

  • Guess how many ChatGPT queries equal everyday items (or vice versa)
  • Switch between three different AI water estimates to see how methodology changes the numbers
  • Compare AI water use to food, clothing, and consumer products
  • Explore the data in different units (gallons, liters, teaspoons)

The tool was built using AI (Stanford's AI Playground) and hosted on GitHub. All data sources are cited and linked. The code is open-source and free to use.

Design principles: Show the data transparently. Let users make their own conclusions. Cite everything. Keep it accessible.

Estimating Water Use: Indirect and Direct Water Consumption

AI water use is difficult to pin down because there are many indirect and direct forms of water consumption, whose inclusion or exclusion dramatically alters estimates. Water is used in three key scopes across the AI model training and query process. Firstly, water is used in datacenter cooling, both on a server-level and facility level, usually through cooling towers, or air cooling systems (Li et al. 3). Cooling towers contaminate water, though water can be recycled for 3-10 cycles, while outside air water involves evaporating water and is usually more water-efficient but at highest demand during hot times when water supplies are most stressed (Li et al. 3). However, new forms of cooling are also being developed, including dry and air cooling which requires little direct water consumption but great deals of electricity, feeding into second-scope estimates (Gutierrez-Chavez).

Second-scope water usage involves the water used to create electricity used to power the data center, which varies depending on how the electricity was generated and how much the center uses (Li et al. 4). Third-scope water use involves the water used in creating the chips and servers themselves, which can sometimes be recycled (Li et al. 4). These uses of water occur both when the model is trained, but also in the operations of each individual query.

The many scopes of water consumption in relation to AI explains the vast variance in water use estimates. For example, Sam Altman's 0.000085 gallons per query estimate takes into account only the water used for the individual query (Altman). A Google study taking into account only first-scope use in cooling a data center suggests that a single Gemini query uses "0.26 milliliters (or about five drops) of water" (Vahdat and Dean). The UT Riverside study, taking into account all scopes of water use, suggests an ultimate result of about 29.3 GPT-3 queries per 500 ml, or 220 queries per gallon of water (Li et al. 5). None of these estimates are wrong, precisely, but they are all conveying vastly different information. When comparing AI water use to other forms of consumption, it is thus crucial to ensure that the things being compared have calculated their impact in the same way.
